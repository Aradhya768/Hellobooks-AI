# Depreciation & Amortization

## What is Depreciation?
Depreciation is the systematic allocation of the cost of a tangible asset over its useful life. Instead of expensing an asset's full cost when purchased, depreciation spreads the expense across the years the asset generates value.

**Applies to**: Physical assets — machinery, vehicles, buildings, equipment, computers

## Why Depreciation Matters
- Matches expense recognition with revenue generation (matching principle)
- Reduces taxable income (tax benefit)
- Reflects true asset value on balance sheet
- Helps plan for asset replacement

## Methods of Depreciation

### 1. Straight-Line Method (Most Common)
Equal depreciation every year.

**Formula**: (Cost - Salvage Value) / Useful Life

**Example:**
- Machine cost: ₹1,00,000
- Salvage value: ₹10,000
- Useful life: 5 years
- Annual depreciation = (1,00,000 - 10,000) / 5 = **₹18,000/year**

### 2. Written Down Value (WDV) / Declining Balance
Depreciation calculated on the remaining book value each year (higher early, lower later).

**Formula**: Book Value × Depreciation Rate

**Example (20% WDV rate):**
| Year | Opening Value | Depreciation | Closing Value |
|------|--------------|--------------|---------------|
| 1 | ₹1,00,000 | ₹20,000 | ₹80,000 |
| 2 | ₹80,000 | ₹16,000 | ₹64,000 |
| 3 | ₹64,000 | ₹12,800 | ₹51,200 |

### 3. Units of Production Method
Depreciation based on actual usage/output.

**Formula**: (Cost - Salvage Value) / Total Units × Units Produced This Period

Best for: Manufacturing equipment where wear correlates to production volume.

## What is Amortization?
Amortization is the same concept as depreciation but applied to **intangible assets** (non-physical).

**Applies to**: Patents, copyrights, trademarks, software licenses, goodwill, franchise agreements

### Amortization vs Depreciation
| Aspect | Depreciation | Amortization |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| Asset Type | Tangible | Intangible |
| Method | Various methods | Usually straight-line |
| Salvage Value | Often considered | Usually zero |
| Examples | Equipment, vehicles | Patents, software |

## EBITDA Explained
**EBITDA = Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation & Amortization**

Since D&A are non-cash expenses, EBITDA provides a clearer picture of operational cash generation. It's widely used in:
- Business valuation
- Comparing companies across industries
- Assessing debt repayment capacity
